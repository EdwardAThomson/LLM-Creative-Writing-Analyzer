The rain above Meridian falls upward.

At dusk, when the city’s towers ignite their window-grids against the bruised violet sky, the droplets rise from streets, gutters, market awnings, the black hair of children, the chrome shoulders of municipal drones. The sentinel answers in a voice made of stolen officials and dead machines.

“The one that made you.”

“If this has activated,” she says, “then the Concord has lied successfully for longer than I deserve.”

“Lio Venn, you are not a citizen accident. You are the last key to a weapon we built and swore never to use. The Assembly will hunt you. The machines will divide over you. The Veil will open. Trust no one who speaks of purity, human or otherwise.”

He is an archive clerk who knows how to authenticate water-damaged petitions and forge cafeteria credits. His hands shake. His knees are weak. The rain climbs.

Above, the orbital rings burn in the night, and beyond them wait the gates, the colonies, the war everyone has been lied into, and the mystery of a power hidden in blood, code, and consciousness.

For the first time in his life, Lio Venn leaves home.

Behind him, on every screen in the Concord, the same forbidden message appears in Amara Quell’s voice.

The Helix heir has awakened.

At dawn, the city of Mirrorglass kneels to the sun.

Its towers bow first: a thousand kilometers of flexing carbon and diamond-pane, bending by fractions as the eastern light spills over the rim of the ocean and catches every window, every skybridge, every public shrine to the Republic. ### The Last Vote of Rain

On the morning the rain votes to fall upward, Mara Venn is kneeling in a field of blue wheat, arguing with a machine that believes it has a soul.

The field lies beyond the eastern walls of Heliopolis, where the city’s towers end abruptly and the old continent remembers soil. Behind Mara, the capital rises in rings of glass and living stone, each tier bright with banners for Election Week: the silver hand of the Assembly, the green spiral of the Terraforming Guilds, the white flame of the Church of Continuance. Above them all hangs the orbital mirror, a false moon catching dawn and pouring it over the planet like mercy.

Before her, the farm stretches toward the ash-gray horizon. Beyond the horizon are the terraforming zones, where the air still tastes of copper and engineered forests crawl across dead basalt. Farther still, invisible in daylight, are the launch arches that send ships into the dark between worlds.

Mara has never been past the equatorial sea.

She tells herself this without bitterness. Everyone on Orison tells themselves the same thing in some form. The democracy promises motion: any citizen may travel, vote, stand for office, petition the planetary AIs, worship any sanctioned god, trade in any market. But promises require fuel, and fuel requires helium, antimatter seed, rare metals, clean water, old debts, new wars, and the permission of people whose names are engraved on buildings.

So Mara farms.

Her family has farmed this strip of engineered land for three generations, bartering protein-grain and medicinal pollen for tools, power cells, memory-thread, and the occasional indulgence from the city replicators. In Heliopolis, abundance is printed from public matter-vaults for those with civic credit. Outside the walls, abundance arrives in rationed cartridges and breaks when dust gets into the nozzle.
