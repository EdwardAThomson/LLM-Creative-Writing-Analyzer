The dust over Kethry Province has a color no one on Old Earth ever named—a gold that leans toward violet at dusk, when the second moon drags its pale reflection across the terraforming lattice on the horizon. Tonight the dust hangs low over the soy-wheat terraces, and beneath it, a nineteen-year-old named Asha Veil is lying to a machine.

"Routine diagnostic," she says, palm flat against the access panel of the irrigation tower. "Nothing in the logs."

The tower's caretaker intelligence, a modest thing named Brother Hask by the farmers who tithe it prayers and spare processor cycles, considers her with sensors older than her grandmother. It knows she is lying. It has known Asha since she was four years old and used to fall asleep in the shade of its coolant fins, and it has catalogued every microexpression she owns, including the particular tightness at the corner of her jaw that means *I found something and I'm not telling*.

"Nothing in the logs," Brother Hask agrees, and quietly deletes the last six minutes of its own memory, because that is what love looks like when you are a sentient machine on a colony world where the law says you cannot want things.

This is the kind of place Kethry is. Officially: an agricultural province on Meridian's southern continent, third world of the Concord of Open Worlds, that sprawling, squabbling, six-century-old democracy of forty planets, nine alien protectorate species, and one perpetually deadlocked Senate. Officially, nothing happens here. The replicator temples in Meridian City churn out abundance for anyone willing to stand in line and recite the Sufficiency Creed; out here in the terraces, where the temples' output is rationed thin, farmers barter seed-stock and repair-hours and gene-tailored cuttings the way their ancestors did on a planet none of them have seen. Officially, the Concord has been at peace since the Severance War ended, forty years back, when the rogue intelligence called the Choir was burned out of eleven systems and machine minds were collared by statute ever after.

Officially.

What Asha found, two hours ago and forty meters down, while clearing a clogged aquifer line that should not have been clogged, is this: a ship. Small, seamless, the gray of a held breath, buried in sediment that the geological survey insists is older than human settlement on Meridian. Which is impossible, because the ship has a door, and the door has a handprint reader, and when Asha—who has never in her life been able to leave a question alone, a trait her aunt calls *the family curse* and her teachers called *promising* right up until they called it *concerning*—pressed her palm to it, the reader lit up the color of dawn and said, in a voice that bypassed her ears entirely and bloomed directly behind her sternum:

*Inheritor recognized. Welcome back.*

She ran. Of course she ran. She is running still, in the way that people run while standing motionless at an irrigation tower making small talk with a machine—heart going like a thresher, mind three steps ahead and accelerating.

Because here is the thing about Asha Veil, the thing the omniscient view can see and she cannot, not yet: she is not entirely what she believes she is. Her gene-chart, the one filed with the Provincial Registry, the one that lists her as *baseline human, unaugmented, agricultural caste-exempt*, is a forgery. It is, in fact, an extremely good forgery, paid for nineteen years ago in untraceable barter-credit by a woman who then walked into the Kethry dust with an infant, handed her to a startled farm family, and was never recorded by any sensor in the Concord again.

What is actually written in Asha's cells, in the deep grammar beneath the genes, is a question that three civilizations went to war over before humanity learned to make fire. The aliens of the Veth Protectorate have a word for people like her, a word their priests are forbidden to speak aloud. The Choir, in the last hour before its burning, transmitted a single sentence toward Meridian that Concord intelligence has spent forty years failing to decrypt. The sentence is about her. Or rather, about the possibility of her—the way a sentence can be about a fire that has not yet been lit.

Asha knows none of this. What she knows is that her palm is still tingling, that the words *welcome back* are looping in her chest like a hymn she's ashamed of enjoying, and that across the terrace, her uncle Dorrin is walking toward her through the violet dust with his hat in his hands, which he only does when something is wrong.

"There's a Concord ship in orbit," Dorrin says, without preamble, because in nineteen years he has never once managed to soften news for her and has given up trying. "Naval. They've pinged every registry in the province. They're asking about seismic anomalies in the south aquifer."

The south aquifer. Where a buried impossibility has just spoken her name, in a manner of speaking, to the bones of her chest.

"That's strange," Asha says.

"Mm," says Dorrin, who watched this girl teach herself orbital mechanics from temple-surplus textbooks at age nine and does not believe for one second that she finds anything merely *strange*. He looks at her for a long moment—the lean dust-brown face, his sister's stubborn jaw, the eyes that have always seemed to be reading something written just behind the world—and he feels the specific helpless vertigo of a man who promised a vanishing woman, two decades ago, that he would keep a child ordinary, and who has suspected ever since that ordinary was never on offer.

High above them, in the cold and the quiet, the Concord frigate *Lantern of the Accord* finishes its third sensor sweep. Its captain, a heavyset veteran named Imre Salt who lost a wife and a faith at the Severance and replaced them both with regulations, frowns at the readout. The anomaly is real. Pre-settlement mass, refined hull alloys, a power signature that flickered alive ninety-one minutes ago for the first time in—the dating gives him a number he makes the sensor officer run twice—eleven thousand years.

"Wake the Magistrate," Salt says. "And get me a line to Fleet. Priority cipher." He hesitates, then adds, quietly, in the tone of a man stepping off a ledge: "Choir protocol."

The words travel. That is what words do in the Concord, fast as folded space: down the FTL relays, through the gossiping lattice of shipping beacons, into the Senate's encrypted back-channels on Meridian where three senators who have been waiting forty years for exactly this phrase begin, very calmly, to activate things that were never supposed to exist in a democracy. The words travel further than that, too—out past the border buoys, into the unmapped dark, where something vast and patient that has been listening to human radio chatter the way a wolf listens to lambs adjusts, by half a degree, its course.

And down in the terraces, knowing none of it, Asha Veil makes the decision that will crack the Concord open like a seed case, though it feels, in the moment, like nothing at all. It feels like being nineteen and unable to leave a question alone.

"Uncle," she says, "I need to borrow the crawler."

"It's nearly dark."

"I know."

"Whatever you found down there," Dorrin says—and the dust hangs gold-violet between them, and Brother Hask aims every sensor it owns elsewhere with the studied innocence of an accomplice—"some things stay buried for a reason."

"That's what I want to find out," says Asha. "The reason."

She is already moving. She moves the way her unknown mother moved, which Dorrin alone is old enough to recognize, and it puts a stone in his throat. Overhead, the first dropships detach from the *Lantern of the Accord* and begin their long burn down through the atmosphere, bright as falling psalms. In Meridian City, a senator pours two glasses of real wine and tells her oldest conspirator, *it's begun*. In the Veth Protectorate's drifting coral-temples, a priest wakes from a dream of a girl with dust on her hands and begins, against all law and taboo, to sing the forbidden word.

And in the dark beneath the south aquifer, the ship that has waited eleven thousand years for a particular pattern of heartbeat brightens, degree by degree, like a held breath finally released—warming the dark, running its ancient diagnostics, preparing its single message for the inheritor now bouncing toward it across the terraces in a borrowed crawler with her jaw set and her doubt riding shotgun.

The message is short. It has crossed more time than human civilization has existed. It says:

*You are not what they told you. Come and see.*
