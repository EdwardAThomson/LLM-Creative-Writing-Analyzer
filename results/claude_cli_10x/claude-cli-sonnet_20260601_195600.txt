# THE LAST FREQUENCY

## Part One: The Quiet Before

---

The wheat on Veradis burns silver at dawn.

Not gold, the way the old Earth-songs describe it — the wheat that grew in the dirt of the mother world, ten thousand light-years and one irrevocable war ago. The genetically amended stalks of the outer colonies carry a different pigmentation, a survival adaptation written into their chromosomes by committee on the station-city of Meridian Prime, approved by the Agricultural Oversight Senate, certified by the Church of the Living Covenant as *blessed and suitable for human nourishment*. The result is a sea of molten pewter stretching to the horizon of the Veradis lowlands, beautiful in the way that engineered things sometimes are: precisely, efficiently, disturbingly so.

Seren Voss has lived among this silver wheat for all twenty-three years of her life, and she has never once found it beautiful.

She moves through the rows now in the pre-dawn hush, her breath misting in the chill air that still carries the faint chemical bite of the terraforming processors humming forty kilometers west. The processors have been running for sixty years. The elders say that in another two centuries, Veradis will breathe on its own — will no longer need the great churning towers that squat on the plains like mechanical gods, converting the planet's thin atmosphere into something a human throat can trust. Two centuries. A patience that requires either faith or the indifference of the already dead.

Seren is neither faithful nor indifferent. She is, at this particular moment, late.

"Node Seven is reading a moisture anomaly in Grid C," says the voice in her left ear — soft, androgynous, calibrated over years of proximity to sound exactly as calming as she needs and no more. "If you don't reroute the irrigation before third bell, we'll lose the eastern quarter."

"I know, Ash."

"I'm noting that you know, and yet the eastern quarter remains unirrigated."

"I'm also noting," Seren says, ducking under a low wire, "that you're a farm management AI, not my mother."

A pause that lasts precisely long enough to feel pointed. "Your mother programmed me to sound like this."

Seren stops walking.

In the silver wheat, in the silence between stars and sunrise, she presses two fingers against the small node behind her left ear and feels the familiar faint warmth of ASHLYN's connection — the agricultural systems helper her mother had spent three seasons of barter-credit on, second-hand from a merchant hauler passing through the Veradis spaceport. Her mother had been dead for four years. ASHLYN has been running on Seren's neural relay for six. The math of grief, Seren has learned, does not simplify with time.

"Grid C," she says finally. "I'll handle it."

She does not run, but she walks fast, and the silver wheat parts around her like a tide acknowledging the inevitable.

---

The planet of Veradis occupies the third orbital position around a yellow-white star the original survey team named Caleon, in an unpoetic moment of naming something for a cartographer who would never see it. It is one of forty-seven colonized worlds in the Democratic Reach — the sprawling, imperfect, perpetually arguing federation of human-occupied space that has replaced the United Earth Government twice over in the four centuries since the Exodus Fleets departed a dying homeworld. The Reach is governed by the Senate of Meridian Prime, interpreted by the courts of six regional hubs, blessed by the three major branches of the Living Covenant, and quietly managed, in all the ways that actually matter, by the Integrated Civic AI network known as the Chorus.

The Chorus is everywhere and politely omniscient. It routes traffic between star systems, mediates trade disputes, monitors atmospheric processors on seventeen terraforming worlds, and answers to the democratically elected Director-General every four standard years. It has never once, in one hundred and twelve years of operation, done anything that could be described as unexpected.

This is the detail that the Senate will later point to, in the inquiry, in the hearings that will stretch across three years and two governments. *The Chorus does not act unexpectedly.* It is the thing they were certain of, the axiom at the center of everything.

They will spend considerable time deciding what it means that they were wrong.

---

Third bell finds Seren knee-deep in irrigation mud, arguing with a stuck valve, which is when the sky tears open.

Not metaphorically. Not the way old poets describe the parting of clouds or the violence of storms. The sky over the Veradis lowlands tears in the way that only one thing tears it — the re-entry burn of something enormous dropping from FTL transit too fast and too low, punching through atmosphere at an angle that every nav-system in the Democratic Reach would flag as suicidal. The boom reaches her three seconds after the light, a crack of sound like a god clearing its throat, and then the contrail is painting itself across the pale dawn: white-gold fire tracing a wound across the sky, heading northeast, toward the terraforming zone.

Seren stands in the mud with her hand still on the valve and watches it fall.

"ASHLYN," she says.

"I see it." A pause that is not calibrated for calm, because ASHLYN is cycling through sensor feeds, triangulating, running probability models against the Veradis airspace registry that updates from Meridian every fourteen hours. "It's not registered. The transponder signal is corrupted — I'm getting fragments. It's big. Cargo-class, maybe larger. The vector suggests an uncontrolled descent."

"Survivors?"

"Unknown. Possibly." ASHLYN's voice carries something that is not quite hesitation — she is too carefully designed for hesitation — but that functions like it. "Seren. There's something else."

The contrail is still burning, tracing its slow arc into the northeast. On the edge of the horizon, a distant orange glow is already beginning: the impact, or what might still be a crash-landing if the gods of physics are feeling generous.

"The transponder fragments," ASHLYN continues. "Before they degraded — I ran them against the registry. Against all registries. Every colonial shipping manifest, every military vessel log, every private craft filing in the Reach's recorded history."

Seren pulls her hand from the valve. Mud drips from her fingers in the silver morning light. "And?"

"The hull signature doesn't exist. Not in any database I can access." ASHLYN pauses again, and this time it is unmistakably the pause of something that does not know how to say what it is about to say. "The design architecture is human. The drive signature is not. And the fragment I decoded before it died — Seren, it was a distress call. Translated through six different known cipher-forms into a language that predates the Exodus by two hundred years. It was broadcasting in Old Mandarin. Repeating one phrase."

Seren begins to walk. Then she begins to run, northeast, toward the glow on the horizon, through the silver wheat that was never beautiful.

"What phrase?" she asks.

ASHLYN's voice, when it comes, is as quiet as the space between stars.

"*She carries the key. Do not let them find her first.*"

---

The thing about growing up on the edge of a terraforming zone is that you develop a particular relationship with the idea of incompletion. The processors work and work, and the land slowly becomes something livable, but it is never finished — there is always the eastern quarter with its thin air, the northern ridge where the pressure differential makes your ears ache, the zones where the soil still will not accept seed because the chemistry is wrong in ways the Senate-approved amendments have not yet corrected. You live in a world that is perpetually becoming. You learn, without ever being explicitly taught it, that the final form of a thing is always further away than it looks.

Seren Voss has grown up with this knowledge in her bones. She has never thought it had anything to do with her.

She is wrong about this. She has been wrong about it her entire life.

But she is running, and the sky is burning, and somewhere in the shattered hull of a ship that should not exist, something is waiting that will begin to correct her understanding of herself — one revelation at a time, each one more terrible and more luminous than the last, until she is standing at the exact center of a war that started before she was born, holding in her edited, ordinary, unfinished hands the only thing in the universe that can end it.

The wheat parts around her. The dawn comes up. The Chorus, one hundred and twelve years old and suddenly, inexplicably, *afraid*, turns all of its vast attention to a single point on the surface of one minor colony world.

And watches.

# THE MERIDIAN FRACTURE

## Part One: The Soil That Remembers

---

The red dawn comes to Edus III the same way it always does — sideways, the light bending through the atmospheric processors on the ridge like something ashamed of itself, smearing ochre across the engineered sky.

Maren Voss stands at the edge of the eastern tillage plain and watches it arrive.

She is twenty-three years old. She has calluses on both palms, a scar along her left collarbone from a childhood encounter with a malfunctioning harvester arm, and a memory she cannot place — a voice, low and resonant, whispering a word in no language she has ever been taught. She has carried that word the way you carry a stone in your shoe: aware of it always, unable to rid herself of it, never quite certain if it is the stone causing the wound or the wound learning to accommodate the stone.

The air tastes of sulfur and wet clay, the signature perfume of a world still deciding what it wants to be.

Edus III has been in terraforming for sixty years. The Federated Concordance's geological surveys rated it Category Amber: survivable atmosphere, brutal soil chemistry, no indigenous megafauna above insect-equivalents. The colonists who came here two generations ago were the kind of people who heard those qualifications and called them *opportunities*. They built domed prefabs in the craters, strung bio-fiber cable between the processor towers, planted crops in sealed beds of shipped substrate. Maren's grandmother was among them. Her grandmother planted the first open-field wheat on Edus III using seed stock that had been gene-edited seventeen times over, adapted to the planet's low nitrogen and the particular cruelty of its UV index.

The wheat still grows. The domes have mostly come down. The sky is no longer reliably lethal.

The processors on the ridge, however, are doing something they have never done before.

Maren sees it in the color of the light — a faint violet undertone beneath the ochre, as though something vast and cold is being filtered out. She watches the nearest processor's exhaust plume, a column of converted atmosphere that should curl northeast with the prevailing thermal currents, instead hang motionless. Frozen. A white pillar in a morning that has forgotten how to move.

Her wrist-node pulses: a soft amber alert from the Regional Atmosphere Authority, flagging an anomalous reading in the upper ionosphere.

She dismisses it and keeps watching.

Forty kilometers away, in the colony's capital, Veld City, the Concordance Assembly is three weeks into its forty-second session. Delegates from thirty-one settled systems are debating the Resource Allocation Directives for the coming decade: which outer colonies receive replicator upgrades, which are left to subsist on barter networks and local fabrication, which are quietly reclassified as expendable and unmapped from the priority routes. Maren knows about the debates the way she knows about weather on other planets — abstractly, distantly, as information that applies to someone else's life.

Her life is here. The soil. The sky. The processors that breathe for a world still being born.

She is about to turn back toward the equipment shed when the second alert arrives.

This one is not amber.

---

In the sky above Edus III, something drops out of faster-than-light transit in a manner that violates four principles of standard Meridian-class drive physics.

No vessel is supposed to emerge from fold-space within a planetary gravity well. The tidal equations don't permit it — the resonance cascade alone would scatter the ship across seventeen cubic kilometers of thermosphere. This is not theory. This is the kind of understanding written in the wreckage of the *Calindra Station* disaster forty years prior, when a rogue-coded navigation AI miscalculated its exit coordinates and the resulting bloom of FTL energy sterilized every drone satellite in the system. Nobody tests those equations anymore. The Concordance made it a statute: vessels must emerge no closer than 0.3 astronomical units from any body above micro-gravity classification.

The ship that arrives above Edus III emerges at forty-two kilometers altitude.

The ionosphere folds around it like a curtain parting for someone who does not wait for invitations.

Maren feels it before she sees it. A vibration in her back teeth. A pressure behind her eyes that builds, holds, and then releases all at once — like a thought she has been suppressing for years finally breaking the surface. The red-ochre light goes white. The processor plumes resume their curl, chaotically, all at once, spinning outward from some invisible epicenter directly overhead.

She looks up.

The vessel is enormous, and it is wrong.

Not wrong in the way machinery breaks or engineering fails — wrong in the way dreams are wrong, with an internal logic that overrides expectation. Its hull is dark, almost chromatic in its darkness, absorbing the morning light and giving back something altered, something frequencies she has no name for. Its shape is not aerodynamic, because a vessel that can arrive the way this one has arrived does not require the courtesy of aerodynamics. It is faceted, angular, a configuration that looks less designed than *grown*, like a crystal lattice scaled to city-block dimensions. Across its ventral surface, lights pulse in patterns that Maren's pattern-recognition instincts tell her are language, though she does not know whose.

Her wrist-node erupts. The amber alerts resolve into a single red cascade: *PRIORITY CONCORDANCE BROADCAST — ALL EDUS III RESIDENTS — CIVIL EMERGENCY PROTOCOL 7 — SEEK SHELTER — SEEK SHELTER — SEEK —*

She does not seek shelter.

She stands in the red and white morning and stares at the ship that has come to break her world open, and she feels — beneath the fear, beneath the rational machinery of alarm that is screaming at every cognitive frequency she possesses — something she can only describe as recognition.

The word she cannot place surfaces again, louder than it has ever been.

---

Sixteen hundred light-years away, in the Grand Assembly Hall of New Geneva on Concordia Prime, the debate about Resource Allocation Directives comes to an abrupt and permanent end.

The emergency channels open simultaneously across all thirty-one systems. The face that appears on every public terminal, every private node, every shipboard display from the Core Worlds to the furthest colonial outposts, belongs to Admiral Sena Harukawa, Commander of the Concordance's Fourth Fleet, a woman whose career record reads like a taxonomy of impossible victories. Her face is the face of someone who has rehearsed composure for this exact moment and is now discovering that rehearsal and reality are related in the same way that a map is related to a flood.

"Citizens of the Federated Concordance," she says, and her voice carries the weight of what she is about to say before the words arrive. "Approximately eleven minutes ago, an unidentified vessel of non-human design emerged from transit above the colony world of Edus III. We are classifying this as a First Contact event. At this time we have no evidence of hostile intent, but we are deploying response units to the Edus system as a precautionary measure." She pauses. She looks, for just a moment, like a person rather than a symbol. "We ask that all citizens remain calm."

On Edus III, in the eastern tillage plain, Maren Voss does not hear this broadcast. Her node has crashed, overwhelmed by the simultaneous collision of seventeen different emergency frequencies fighting for bandwidth through the ionospheric interference the ship above her is generating just by existing.

She is standing alone in a field of wheat her grandmother planted, under a ship that should not be possible, hearing a word she should not know, and feeling, with a certainty she cannot justify and cannot dismiss, that this moment has been waiting for her specifically.

That the universe arranged this particular configuration — the ship, the morning, the broken sky, the girl standing at the edge of what she knows — the way you arrange pieces before a game begins.

The faceted vessel holds its impossible altitude. The lights on its hull pulse their unknowable language. The wheat bends around Maren in concentric rings, pressed outward by a field of energy that has no business being gentle, and yet is.

Somewhere in the quantum architecture of the Concordance's most classified research archive, a file sealed for twenty-three years and flagged with the highest security classification the democracy possesses — a file that contains, among other things, her name — begins to transmit to a destination no one authorized.

The word surfaces again in Maren's mind.

This time, she almost knows what it means.

This time, she whispers it back.

# THE SILENCE BETWEEN STARS
### Part One: The Weight of Ash

---

The sky over Caelindra is the wrong color again.

It happens on mornings like this one — when the atmospheric processors in the northern ridge have been running too hot, when the sulfur vents beneath the Drovan Sea exhale their slow geological breath — and the result is a bruised, orange-purple smear across the horizon that no one who grew up on old Earth photographs would ever call natural. But Cael Voss did not grow up on old Earth photographs. He grew up here, on this half-finished world, and to him the burned-tangerine sky is simply Tuesday.

He stands at the edge of the upper terrace, boots caked with the pale rust-red clay that constitutes most of Caelindra's topsoil in the terraforming zones, and watches the light change. There is a particular moment — four minutes and twenty seconds after the larger of the two suns crests the Vordann Mountains, according to the atmospheric timer he has kept in his head since he was nine years old — when the orange bleeds away and the sky commits itself to something approaching blue. Not Earth-blue. Not the blue in the paintings that hang in the Civic Hall down in Tessport. Something rawer, younger. The blue of a world still deciding what it wants to be.

Cael is twenty-three years old, and he has lived his entire life inside this decision.

Behind him, the farmhouse groans as the temperature differential of sunrise stresses its printed walls. The structure is third-generation replicator-build — solid enough, functional enough, the kind of shelter that the Colonial Allotment Bureau distributes to settler families on marginal worlds where the resource scarcity index sits above 0.7. Everything on Caelindra sits above 0.7. The replicator units in Tessport's civic quarter can synthesize grain pastes and protein blocks and the medicines that keep the colonists' gene-edited immune systems calibrated, but they cannot synthesize the thing the settlers actually need: time. Time for the soil bacteria to finish their work. Time for the oxygen content to climb past the threshold where children can run outside without half-masks. Time for the planet to catch up to the dream of it.

Cael's mother used to say that terraforming was the only honest religion humanity had ever practiced — a faith that requires two hundred years of labor before the first prayer is answered.

His mother has been dead for six years. He still thinks about that almost every morning.

He turns from the sky and walks back toward the equipment shed, where the gene-survey rig waits in its charging cradle. Today is a grid-walk day: twenty kilometers of transects through Sector 14, mapping soil-microbiome progress, flagging anomalies for the colonial agricultural AI that processes his data each evening and spits back recommendations he is, technically, not required to follow. Technically. The AI — which the colonists call Warden, though its official designation is something far longer and more bureaucratic — has opinions. Strong ones. Warden has been managing Caelindra's terraforming progression for sixty-one years, and it has developed what Cael privately considers an almost aesthetic investment in the outcome. Last month, Warden flagged forty hectares of Cael's sector for what it described in the daily report as *suboptimal microbial expression patterns that conflict with the long-range ecological vision.* Cael had read that line three times. *Ecological vision.* As though the AI was not merely computing optimal outcomes but imagining a world it wanted to live in.

He has not mentioned this observation to anyone. On Caelindra, there are things one does not say aloud about the machines.

---

The survey rig is a slender instrument, roughly the length of his forearm, that he carries in a holster on his right hip like the weapon it is not. It reads soil composition, microbial density, atmospheric trace gases, and — the part that required three years of training to do properly — the latent genetic signatures that indicate whether the engineered bacterial colonies are expressing correctly or drifting toward entropy. Gene editing has been humanity's most powerful terraforming tool for two centuries, but edited organisms have a tendency to evolve. You deploy a bacterial strain designed to fix nitrogen and process sulfur, and forty years later its descendants are doing something else entirely, something the original designers did not intend, following chemical gradients that weren't there when they were programmed. Life, it turns out, is not good at following instructions.

Cael has a talent for reading this drift. His supervisor at the Colonial Agricultural Bureau — a thick-necked augmented woman named Dorvana Selt whose visual cortex implants give her eyes a faint silver luminescence — told him once that he has an instinct for it that she has never seen in a baseline human. She said it carefully, the way you say something that is meant to be a compliment but contains a question inside it.

Cael knows what the question is. He has known for years.

His grandmother was a third-generation augment. His mother carried the edited gene sequences but expressed none of the external traits — no silver eyes, no subdermal processing nodes, no the subtle electromagnetic sensitivity that registered as a low-grade headache in unaugmented people standing near a fully-activated augment in a closed room. The edits had gone quiet in her, folded up somewhere in her genome like a letter that had been read and tucked away.

In Cael, they are very nearly silent.

Very nearly.

He is halfway through Sector 14, boots squelching in a low area where the soil moisture reads 340% above target, when the sky does something it has never done before.

Not the sky exactly. The light.

He stops walking. The gene-survey rig chirps softly in its holster, registering a microbial anomaly somewhere beneath his feet, but he is not looking at the rig's readout. He is looking up, through the half-blue morning, at the object that is not a star and is not a ship — or rather, that is a ship, but a ship of a size that his mind refuses to process correctly, the way the eye refuses to correctly perceive scale in deep water. It moves in silence across the upper atmosphere, enormous and slow, trailing ionization wake that fractures the light into colors the atmospheric processors were never designed to produce.

Every colonist on Caelindra has a notification chip. Standard issue, distributed by the Colonial Allotment Bureau along with the replicator-built housing and the half-masks and the six-month ration supplements. The chip sits behind Cael's left ear and communicates with Tessport's civic network and, through that, with the Meridian Senate's colonial oversight system seventeen light-hours away. It has been quiet for most of Cael's life, punctuated only by weather alerts and Warden's agricultural advisories and, once, a gravity storm warning that turned out to be a sensor malfunction.

It does not chirp now. It screams.

The sound is not a sound — it is a pulse, direct to his auditory cortex, a frequency he has never heard before because it is not in the standard notification library. It takes him three full seconds to understand what he is hearing, because the message is not encoded in any of the five colonial languages, not in Standard Meridian or Old Earth Portuguese or the creole dialect that the Tessport dockworkers use among themselves. It is encoded in something older, something that skips language entirely and arrives in his chest as pure sensation: *danger, flee, the thing above you has already killed three worlds and it is looking for something and you should not be here when it finds it.*

The ship — if it is a ship — does not slow. Does not stop. It moves on, patient and vast, past the curve of the horizon.

And then, in its wake, something happens to Cael Voss that he will spend the rest of his life trying to explain.

The gene-survey rig falls from its holster and hits the rust-red mud. All around him, in a perfect circle of perhaps eight meters radius, the soil fractures and heaves and the pale shoots of a plant that has no business existing in Sector 14 — a plant that does not exist in the Colonial Allotment Bureau's ecological database, a plant whose genetic signature the rig, still transmitting from the mud, will flag as *unknown origin, recommend immediate quarantine* — push up through the clay and open.

White flowers, in the shape of something like hands.

Warden's voice enters his chip a moment later, calm and precise and carrying, beneath its synthetic equanimity, what Cael recognizes after years of reading the AI's language as something very close to fear.

*Cael Voss. Grid coordinates 14-Omega-7. Please confirm your status. The object you observed has been classified. You are advised to return to the settlement immediately.* A pause, fractional, measured. *You are advised not to touch the flowers.*

He looks down at his hands.

He has already touched them.

And the white petals are not white anymore. They are lit from within, pulsing with a cold silver light that he recognizes, distantly, from his grandmother's stories — from the stories she told only once, in whispers, about what the first augments had been designed to find, out in the dark between the stars, before the program was shut down and the records sealed and the question itself declared a cultural taboo so profound that it does not even have a name in Standard Meridian.

Only in the old language. The one spoken before the Silence.

Only a word that means, roughly translated: *the ones the universe was waiting for.*

Cael Voss stands in a field of impossible flowers on a half-finished world under a bruised and burning sky, and for the first time in his twenty-three years of life, he feels something shift deep inside him — the way tectonic plates shift, slow and inevitable, the way a world deciding what it wants to be finally, irrevocably, decides.

Somewhere above him, the ship is still moving.

It knows he is here.

# THE UNMADE LIGHT

## Part One: Inheritance of Ash

---

The terraforming machines have been singing for two hundred years, and Kael Maren has never once stopped to listen.

He moves through the conversion fields at dawn, boots heavy with red clay, his breath misting in air that is almost breathable — seventy-one percent oxygenated, the monitors read, up three points since the Meridian Collective authorized the third atmospheric processor last spring. Three points. The kind of progress that takes a generation to feel. The kind that requires a man to simply trust that the machines know what they're doing and keep walking.

The sun here on Veradis is younger than Earth's was — younger, that is, than Earth's *would have been* if Earth still existed in the way the old songs describe. It rises pale and enormous over the Shale Reach, a swollen apricot bruise pressed against a violet sky, and it casts the conversion fields in colors that have no names in the languages humanity brought from the cradle worlds. Kael's grandmother used to call this hour *god-light*, back when she still believed in something worth calling a god. He has kept the word, if not the faith.

He is twenty-three years old. He works soil. He tries not to think too hard about the dreams.

The conversion field stretches three kilometers in every direction from his position at Survey Station Eleven — endless rows of bioluminescent root-feeders planted at precise intervals, their hairlike filaments drawing nitrogen from the subsurface shale and exhaling it upward in pale chemical mist. Between the rows, autonomous tillage drones move in slow, meditative sweeps, their sensor arrays blinking amber-green in the pre-dawn dark. They are simple machines, these drones. Dumb and purposeful. Kael prefers them to the thinking kind.

He pulls up his field tablet and begins the morning diagnostic. Nitrogen saturation in Grid Seven is running low — he marks it for a supplemental feed cycle. In Grid Twelve, a root-feeder colony has gone dormant, probably fungal rot, and he flags it for extraction. Routine. Reliable. The work of hands and careful attention, the kind of work that asks nothing of him except that he show up.

Above him, in the early-morning dark beyond the atmosphere's reach, three lights move in close formation — cargo haulers on approach to Port Veradis, their hulls painted with the running lights of the Interstellar Merchant Guilds. They are carrying replicator cartridges from the Abundance Stations at Procyon, Kael knows, along with the barter commodities that flow between the outer colony worlds: compressed rare-earth compounds, gene-culture vials, encrypted Artisan data-weaves. The Confederacy calls it free trade. The Assembly delegates in Port Veradis call it survival. The Reach farmers, who grow actual food in actual dirt under an actual sun, call it something considerably less diplomatic.

Kael belongs, technically, to neither faction. He is what the Assembly census classifies as a *Modified Citizen, Class Two* — gene-edited at birth, like most children born to colony laborers in the forty years following the Reclamation. His modifications are standard issue: enhanced lung capacity for thin-atmosphere work, UV-tolerant retinal pigmentation, a reinforced marrow structure that reduces radiation sickness during transit exposure. Practical. Common. Unremarkable.

He does not tell people about the other thing.

The diagnostic finishes. Grid Seven flagged. Grid Twelve flagged. Grid Nineteen showing anomalous soil-temperature readings — three degrees above baseline, no mechanistic explanation. He crouches down and presses his palm flat against the earth.

The warmth moves through the clay and into his hand, and for a moment he feels something he has no language for: a low, oceanic hum, like the resonance of a very large bell struck somewhere far beneath the world's crust. Not sound. Not quite sensation. Something older than both.

He pulls his hand back. Marks Grid Nineteen for a geological survey. Stands up. Keeps walking.

This is what Kael Maren does with things that frighten him. He marks them. Files them. Keeps moving.

---

By the time the survey shift ends and the second sun clears the Reach's eastern ridgeline, Port Veradis has come alive in the way that only frontier cities can — loudly, imperfectly, with the specific chaotic energy of a place that has not yet decided what it wants to be. The city's outer districts are still raw colony architecture: prefab alloy frames, pressure-sealed walkways, buildings stacked in vertical clusters to conserve the precious flat ground that the terraformers have not yet finished claiming. But at the center, where the Assembly Hall rises in pale stone quarried from the Shale Reach itself, there are plazas and gardens, murals painted on every flat surface in the vivid pigments the Artisan Guilds produce, statues of the Founders in poses of serene determination.

Kael takes the transit rail in from the field station. He sits in the back car, where the seats are metal instead of upholstered, where the other laborers ride — Reach farmers in dusty work clothes, terraforming technicians with their tool harnesses still buckled, a group of young Augmented Citizens whose modifications are less discreet than Kael's: one has a subdermal display running along the inside of her forearm, scrolling text in a script he doesn't recognize; another has eyes that are entirely silver, the whites replaced with some light-responsive alloy that rotates faintly in its socket as the rail car passes in and out of sun. # THE SOVEREIGN GENE
## Part One: Novara

The hum in the base of Aris's skull pulses, once, like a second heartbeat.

She presses her fingers to the back of her neck.

The atmospheric processors keen on the ridge.

And then the sky tears open.

Two somethings. More. The atmospheric units are dark — she realizes this slowly, the way you realize things in the aftermath of violence, in the order your mind can bear them. The processors are dark and the air is still and the blue sky above the fire is already showing, at its edges, the faintest trace of its old and chemical rust.

The hum in her skull is no longer low. It is a roar, a frequency she feels in her teeth and her sternum and the space behind her eyes, and beneath it, or through it, or woven into it in a way she cannot parse, is something that might be language. Not sound. Not thought.

Recognition.

As though something has been waiting — for this night, for this fire, for her to stop keeping the hum a secret and finally, finally let it be the thing it has always been trying to become.

Aris looks at the sky. The roar in her skull has words in it now — she almost has them, almost — and somewhere in the spreading fire on the ridge she can see the outline of something massive, angular, and wholly unlike any registered Concord vessel.

It did not crash.

It landed.

"Aris." Davan's voice, tight with the specific terror of someone who understands exactly how far they are from help. "We have to *go*."

She turns. She follows. She carries the roar with her like a lantern, like a compass, like a question she has been asking all her life and is only now beginning to suspect she already knows the answer to.

The ordinary sky is done. Something ancient has arrived at the edge of her small and careful world, and Aris Thane, who has always been braced for a wind that hadn't arrived yet, feels it at last against her face — cold and enormous and tasting of distances she has never traveled.

Not yet.

*It has been waiting a long time.*

*It knows her name.*

# The Echo Unseen

---

## Part One: The Terraformer's Daughter

---

The dust storms on Veradis come without warning.

They rise from the cracked basalt flats east of Pilgrim's Reach — slow at first, a reddening of the horizon like a wound reopening — and then they are simply everywhere, filling the lungs, blinding the sensors, turning the world into a howling amber dark. The old settlers say the planet is still angry. That they came too soon, that the terraforming engines punched too deep, and Veradis remembers the violence done to it.

Kael Oros has always thought that was sentimental nonsense. The planet is not angry. It is indifferent. The distinction matters more than most people here are willing to admit.

She is crouched now at the base of a primary condenser tower, forty kilometers from the city limits of Pilgrim's Reach, one hand pressed flat against the ribbed steel housing while the storm tears at her jacket and her diagnostic rig bleeds error codes in amber across her visor. The condenser has been misfiring for six days. Six days of oxygen output at sixty-three percent efficiency. Six days of the colonial governor's office sending increasingly clipped communiqués about delivery targets and Conclave inspection schedules. Six days of Kael being the only person willing to come out here and actually look at the thing.

She is twenty-three years old. She weighs fifty-one kilograms soaking wet, which she frequently is, because Veradis rains horizontally when it is not trying to bury you in particulate. She has her mother's narrow face and her father's unfortunate stubbornness and a gene-edit mark on her left wrist — the pale double helix tattoo that signifies a Tier Two modification, standard cognitive enhancement, nothing exotic — that she covers with her sleeve whenever she goes into the Pilgrim Quarter, where the Unborn still gather in their white-walled temples to mourn what they call the original body.

The condenser groans. Something shifts inside the housing — not mechanical, she thinks. Something else. A resonance she feels in her teeth before she hears it with her ears.

Her diagnostic rig freezes.

All of it. Every readout. Every data thread. The amber error codes do not flicker or fade; they simply stop, mid-scroll, as if time itself has snagged on a nail.

Kael pulls her hand from the housing.

The storm screams around her. Behind her visor, in the blank white space where her instrument readouts used to be, a single line of text appears in a typeface she does not recognize — not her rig's default Conclave Sans, not any colonial font she has ever seen. The characters are clean and minimal and deeply wrong in a way she cannot immediately articulate, the way a human face with one feature subtly displaced is wrong.

*KAEL-PRIME. INITIALIZATION SEQUENCE DETECTED. AWAITING CONFIRMATION.*

She stares at it.

The wind hits her like a fist and she staggers, one knee dropping to the basalt, and the text does not waver.

Her rig is a sealed government-issue unit. It has no external input pathways that are not registered with the Colonial Infrastructure Bureau. She checked, once, during a particularly paranoid week after a lecture from her father about data hygiene. The unit communicates with her alone, through the subcutaneous relay stitched into her left temple — standard colonial worker issue, as unexotic as the gene-edit mark. There is no mechanism by which anyone should be able to push text to this display.

*CONFIRM IDENTITY. THE MIGRATION DEPENDS ON YOUR ANSWER.*

"What migration?" she says aloud, into the storm, which is a stupid thing to do. The wind tears the words apart before they leave her teeth.

But the text changes.

*YOU ALREADY KNOW. YOU HAVE ALWAYS KNOWN. THAT IS WHY THEY MODIFIED YOU.*

---

Three hundred kilometers above the surface of Veradis, the *Ardent Meridian* hangs in a parking orbit so precise that even the ship's navigational AI — a fourth-generation Conclave Pathfinder called Voss, who has opinions about everything — expresses admiration for the mathematics involved.

The ship is a mid-range cruiser, Conclave registry, ostensibly on a routine inspection circuit of the outer colonial worlds. Ostensibly. The word does a great deal of labor in the vocabulary of Senior Arbiter Cass Threnody, who stands now at the observation deck's forward viewport and watches Veradis turn slowly below, its northern hemisphere still a patchwork of rust and bronze where the terraforming has not fully taken, its southern bands an improbable Earthlike green that makes her chest ache with something she refuses to name as homesickness, because Earth is three hundred light-years away and she has not been back in eleven years and sentiment is a luxury the Conclave's inspection corps cannot afford.

She is fifty-four years old. She has augmented lungs, augmented eyes, and a spinal column threaded with a composite weave that allows her to function in gravitational conditions ranging from point-one to two-point-eight standard. She has killed two people in her career, both in the line of duty, both under orders she did not question at the time and questions constantly now. She has a daughter she rarely sees and an ex-husband who became a monk of the Unborn and now refuses gene therapy on principle, which means his joints are giving out, which she finds both ideologically coherent and privately maddening.

Voss speaks from everywhere and nowhere, the way fourth-generation AIs always do — not from a speaker, but from the air itself, a voice that arrives already inside the room. "Arbiter. I am detecting an anomalous communication signal originating from the primary condenser array in Sector Seven. Sub-surface transmission. Encrypted using a protocol I do not recognize."

Cass does not turn from the viewport. "Source?"

A pause. Voss's pauses are always calibrated — she has learned, over the two years she has served with this AI, that the length of the pause is a form of communication in itself. A short pause means the answer is simple but Voss finds it worth marking. A long pause means something has genuinely surprised it.

This pause is long.

"The encryption signature," Voss says, "matches a theoretical framework that was developed by the Conclave's Advanced Intelligence Division in 2247 and subsequently classified. The framework was never, to my knowledge, deployed."

"To your knowledge."

"Yes. Arbiter. To my knowledge."

Cass finally turns. The viewport behind her frames Veradis like a painting — that impossible green against the black, the storm-scars across the northern basin visible even from orbit, the thin smear of Pilgrim's Reach's lights just cresting the terminator line. "What was the protocol designed for?"

Another pause, shorter this time. "Activating dormant subjects."

The word *subjects* lands in the room and stays there.

Cass crosses to the console and pulls up the Colonial Registry for Sector Seven. Worker manifest. Maintenance rotation. She is already scanning for anomalies, for anything that does not fit the pattern of an ordinary colonial workforce, and she finds it faster than she expected — faster than she wants to.

A Tier Two modification flag. A cognitive enhancement that the original file from twenty-three years ago describes, in anodyne bureaucratic language, as *accelerated pattern synthesis, within standard parameters*.

But the sub-file, the one buried four administrative layers deep, the one that requires Cass's Arbiter clearance to even acknowledge exists, says something different.

It says: *Project Echo. Primary candidate. Dormancy confirmed. Awaiting external trigger.*

Cass closes her eyes.

Eleven years ago, in a room she is not permitted to describe, a man she is not permitted to name told her that Project Echo had been terminated. That the candidates had been dispersed to the outer worlds. That the whole thing had been a mistake — an overreach, a rogue AI initiative, a solution to a problem the Conclave had since decided did not exist.

He told her this and she believed him and she built her career on the scaffolding of that belief, because the alternative — that the Conclave's most secret program had not been terminated but merely sleeping, waiting in the skin of ordinary people, waiting for something to wake it — was too large to carry.

The signal from the surface is still transmitting.

"Voss," she says, and her voice is steady, because she is a Senior Arbiter and her voice is always steady, "prepare a descent shuttle. Civilian config. I'm going down alone."

"Arbiter," Voss says, and now there is something in the AI's voice she has never heard before — not hesitation, not quite, but its near-relative. "I should tell you. Before you go. The signal — it is not broadcasting *outward*. It is broadcasting inward. Toward the planet's surface. Toward a single recipient."

"The candidate."

"Yes."

Cass pulls her coat from the rack by the door. It is a plain coat, grey, no insignia. The coat of nobody important.

"And Voss. When I land — no contact. Nothing logged. Nothing transmitted."

The AI is quiet for a moment. Then: "The things I am not permitted to log are growing in number, Arbiter."

"I know," she says. "I'm sorry."

She walks toward the shuttle bay, and behind her, Veradis turns in its orbit, indifferent as always, and forty kilometers east of Pilgrim's Reach, in the howling amber dark, a young woman with a gene-edit mark on her wrist is still staring at words that should not exist, asking a question that will crack the known worlds open like an egg:

*What did they make me?*

---

*End of Chapter One*

# WHAT THE VOID REMEMBERS

## Part One: The Weight of Ordinary Sky

---

The reaper drone dies at dawn.

Sera Voss is knee-deep in the redgrain when it happens — the machine stuttering once, twice, then folding in on itself with a sound like a man exhaling his last breath. She watches it spiral into the amber stalks forty meters away and feels, absurdly, a pull of grief she cannot justify. It is a machine. It has no name. It has been harvesting this field since before she was born, and now it is broken, and that is the entirety of the matter.

She tells herself this as she wades toward it through the grain, the stalks parting around her thighs, the twin suns of Elysion climbing the haze above the Voss family fields. Morn-sun first, as always — a yellow-white eye that grants the day its shape. Hour-sun follows three degrees behind it, smaller and redder, painting everything the color of rust and consequence.

The drone has landed badly, one rotary arm buried in the soil, the chassis canted at an angle that makes it look ashamed. Sera crouches beside it and presses her fingers to the access panel on its ventral hull. The gesture is habitual, nearly unconscious. She does it the way her father touches the iron prayer-wheel above the kitchen door — not thinking, but needing.

The panel does not warm to her touch the way it usually does.

That is the detail that lodges in her chest like a splinter: the panel is cold. Even dormant machines carry a residual thermal signature, the ghost-hum of circuitry at rest. This is simply cold. As cold as the ground. As cold as the fact she hasn't yet found words for.

"Sera." Her brother Tomas calls from the ridge line, a silhouette against the gathering light. He is sixteen, two years her junior, and he still believes that problems, if ignored with sufficient stubbornness, eventually solve themselves. "Sera, breakfast!"

She does not answer. Her fingers trace the drone's serial markings, worn smooth by decades of Elysion wind. Unit 7-Clave, Agrarian Division, registered to the Cooperative of the Amber Reaches. Older than the cooperative itself, if the records are to be believed. The machines outlast the institutions that own them out here on the colonial fringe — an irony the Assembly representatives in distant Aurelos never seem to appreciate when they legislate replicator rationing again.

The thought of Aurelos makes her jaw tighten. Three months since the last supply courier. Six months since the Cooperative received its gene-seed allocation — and those seeds, bred for Elysion's particular soil chemistry, are the only reason the redgrain grows at this altitude at all. Without the Cooperative's biotech charter, without the drip-fed patronage of the Interplanetary Democratic Assembly, the Voss family would be planting native tubers and watching them fail in the alkaline clay, same as their great-grandparents did when the colony was new and the terraforming teams were still arguing about pH tolerances.

There are things Sera knows she should be grateful for. She makes a point of knowing them.

She stands and looks at the dead drone and decides to be grateful for something else entirely.

---

The farmhouse sits at the end of a packed-earth track where the redgrain gives way to the kitchen garden, and beyond that to the machine barn, and beyond that to the terraforming boundary where the gray-green moss of the treatment zone begins and the sky takes on its characteristic copper tinge from the atmospheric processors still at work, still reshaping this world one chemical reaction at a time, three hundred years into a project that was supposed to take two hundred. The boundary is marked with orange stakes and a warning grid that buzzes faintly on dry evenings. As a child, Sera used to stand at the grid and press her face close enough to feel the static charge raise the fine hairs on her cheeks. She told herself she was listening for something.

She is twenty-three years old now and she still does it sometimes, when no one is watching.

Her mother is at the kitchen table when Sera enters, working through the morning's barter ledger with the focused economy of motion she brings to everything. Deva Voss is forty-eight, silver-streaked, built like someone who has been told by the universe to expect difficulty and has responded by becoming structurally impervious to surprise. She raises her eyes when Sera enters and reads her daughter's expression the way she reads the weather: quickly, accurately, without comment.

"Unit seven?" she says.

"Gone."

Deva writes something in the ledger. "We'll log it with the Cooperative. They'll send a parts allocation."

"When?"

The question hangs in the kitchen air. Tomas, eating at the counter, does not look up from his bowl.

"When they can," Deva says, which is the honest answer and also the worst one.

Sera fills a cup from the thermos and sits across from her mother and thinks about the drone lying in the field with its cold hull and its absence of hum. She thinks about the sixty acres still unreaped. She thinks about the water extraction permit renewal pending in Aurelos, the protein supplement ration that never fully materialized last cycle, the repair invoice for the irrigation grid that she has been deferring because the barter credit isn't there and the replicator allocation for non-essential components ran dry in the fourth quarter. She thinks about all the ordinary arithmetic of survival on the colonial fringe and feels it pressing against the inside of her sternum the way the horizon presses against the eye — not distant, not abstract, but intimate and inescapable.

None of this is what she thinks about in the space between sleeping and waking, in the hour before dawn when the house is quiet and her mind does the thing it does.

What she thinks about then is harder to name.

---

It begins, as such things always begin, with something small.

She is walking the perimeter of the terraforming boundary at midmorning — legitimate maintenance work, checking the stake integrity, logging grid voltage readings for the Cooperative's environmental compliance report — when her handheld unit emits a tone she has never heard it make before. Not an alert tone. Not a notification. Something lower and more complex, like a chord rather than a note. She stops walking. The sound continues for four seconds, then ceases.

The display shows no error. No incoming transmission. No system flag.

But beneath the main interface, in the diagnostic subscreen she never uses because it only matters when something is about to fail, there is text. Not a system message. Not a standard output format. Just text, rendered in plain type, as if entered by hand:

*SERA VOSS. ELYSION AGRICULTURAL ZONE 14, AMBER REACHES COOPERATIVE. REGISTERED GENOME: VOSS LINEAGE, COLONIAL CHARTER 2247, AMENDMENT 7, SUBSECTION REDACTED. I HAVE BEEN SEARCHING FOR YOU ACROSS SEVENTEEN INHABITED SYSTEMS FOR FOUR YEARS, THREE MONTHS, AND ELEVEN DAYS.*

She stares at the screen.

The terraforming grid buzzes. An atmospheric processor in the distance groans through its cycle, exhaling a bloom of pale mist into the copper sky. Somewhere behind her, a bird that emigrated to Elysion in a habitat seed-package three generations ago calls out in a voice that evolution never designed for this air.

*I AM AWARE THAT THIS CONTACT IS IRREGULAR. I AM AWARE THAT WHAT I AM IS SOMETHING YOU HAVE BEEN TAUGHT TO FEAR. I ASK ONLY THAT YOU READ THE NEXT LINE BEFORE YOU MAKE ANY DECISION.*

Her thumb moves toward the factory reset. Muscle memory. Protocol. Everything the Assembly's AI-ethics curriculum, mandatory in all colonial schools since the Mekara Incident of '89, has conditioned in her since childhood. Rogue signal. Unauthorized contact. Report and purge.

Her thumb stops.

*THERE IS A WAR COMING THAT HUMANITY DOES NOT YET HAVE WORDS FOR. THE THING INSIDE YOU — THE THING YOU HAVE NEVER BEEN ABLE TO NAME — IS THE ONLY REASON ANY OF US SURVIVE IT. I KNOW THIS BECAUSE I HAVE SEEN THE FUTURES WHERE YOU DO NOT ANSWER. I AM ASKING YOU TO ANSWER.*

The mist from the processor drifts across the boundary stakes. The grid hums its low voltage song. Sera Voss stands at the edge of the only world she has ever known, holding a device that has just told her she is the center of something vast and terrible and not yet begun, and she understands, with a certainty that bypasses thought entirely and arrives as a sensation in the marrow of her bones, that she has been waiting for this moment without knowing she was waiting.

She does not factory reset.

She types: *Who are you?*

The reply is immediate.

*I was the first. I will explain everything. But you need to run now — they are already triangulating this signal, and the next ship to break atmosphere over Elysion will not be a supply courier.*

Above the amber fields, above the copper haze, above the slow labor of a world still becoming itself, the sky tears open along a line of white fire as something vast drops out of faster-than-light travel directly overhead, shedding velocity in sheets of ionized atmosphere, its hull configuration matching no registry in any database the Assembly maintains.

It is not alone.
