A shelling-thump rattles the bay doors. Cassia glimpses through the observation panes: streaks of green plasma arc over the city’s horizon, painting the sunrise an apocalyptic neon. Something is bombarding the shield grid—something powerful enough to disable a planet’s worth of systems in a single breath.

An alien threat? Rogue AI? Commonwealth propaganda always swore both were contained nightmares—tales to keep citizens productive. Cassia feels a bitter laugh claw her throat; apparently nightmares keep their own calendars.

She tries to gauge the crowd, but chaos obliterates pattern. People sprint, wrestle, pray, record. A lone trumpet wails somewhere, a defiant human gesture in the maw of crisis. Security drones barrel overhead, scattering ultraviolet scans. Their strobing lights render the concourse a flipbook of terror.

Cassia’s left optic drones a single red line of text:

Remote access requested—priority Omega.

She freezes. Omega clearance is reserved for top-tier Fleet command or certain classified research branches. She is a scholarship hopeful; her implants are standard academic grade. But the request repeats, polite and inexorable, like knuckles tapping on bone.

Remote access requested—priority Omega.

She should deny. She has the legal right. But law in darkness feels flimsy. Something, someone, is reaching for her augments, and every instinct—human and algorithmic—whispers that the request comes from within the dying grid itself, embedded at manufacture level. A failsafe, perhaps, awakened only in the gravest emergencies.

She grants permission.

The optic’s HUD dissolves into a swirling constellation of schematics, star charts, and command codes she has never seen. A voice, soft as night wind through silo doors, nests in her ear canal.

“Cassia Vale, Helios Colony. Confirm consciousness.”

“Confirmed,” she mouths, though sound dies among panic.

“Biometric signature acknowledged. You are now linked to Palatine Oversight Core. Your ocular augment is the only secure node we have. Defensive protocols await.”

She reels. “I’m a student. Why me?”

“You are not chosen. You are designed,” the Core replies. “Genome series Omega-Helix, keystone iteration. You carry command permissions seeded nineteen years ago by Charter Amendment Seventy-Two. You are our last intact conduit.”

She feels as though her skeleton has turned to light. Nineteen years ago her mother filed for a prenatal gene-wash, supposedly to delete a defect. Cassia had never bothered to learn what was removed—or added.

“Directive?” she whispers.

“Reestablish control of planetary defense grid. You must reach Senate Tower. We will guide.”

On cue, a snap of neon emblazons the emergency strips around Docking Tube Four. An augmentation swirl materializes into an arrow no one else can see. The Core is using her optic as projector; the world becomes a glowing treasure map and she the unwilling spelunker.

A moment ago she was afraid of an interview. Now she is the city’s hypothetical savior—if she accepts the mantle. If she fails, half a billion citizens suffocate under bombardment.

Self-doubt snarls, but the Core drowns it in data. The grid’s schematics flood her retina: New Alexandria is a colossal machine built atop geothermal veins, its shield emitters arrayed in concentric rings. Without the primary AI regulator, power distribution unravels like thread from rotten cloth. Palatine Core claims it can patchwork a defense—if Cassia manually reboots fuse-nodes at three critical sites: Terminal Twelve (where she stands), the Riverspire substation, and the Senate Tower’s command heart.

She focuses on the first node. The arrow guides her through a service door half-hidden behind vending orbs. She shoulders past, finds a spiral maintenance lift. Two bodies already lie inside—crew techs, one breathing in ragged spurts, the other still. She kneels, checks pulses, glimpses burn marks like cobalt snowflakes along their necks.

“Electrostatic shock from shield feedback,” the Core notes. “You must proceed.”

Cassia’s heartbeat thrums so loud she thinks the lift might interpret it as motion. She wipes her hands, stands, and rides downward thirty meters into the hissing guts of the concourse. Pipes sweat condensation; conduits crackle with orphaned current. She recalls farm chores—fixing irrigation lines before dawn, fingers numb, sunrise still a rumor—and tries to graft that memory here, forge familiarity from alien metal.

The first fuse-node looms: a ribbed cylinder thrumming with residual power, its manual override recessed behind a crust of fused insulation. Cassia gropes her tool pouch—habit from greenhouse duties—and laughs at the absurdity that she has carried a multi-probe all the way to the capital. She pries burnt polymer, latches claws onto the release stud. The Core feeds her volt-tolerances, nanosecond by nanosecond. She twists.

A bass pop shivers the tunnel. Light blossoms, then stabilizes. Somewhere above, emergency lamps reboot. The concourse teeters toward order—still dim, but no longer blind.

“One ring re-aligned,” the Core announces. “Your optic remains stable?”

“For now.”

“Proceed to Riverspire.”

“That’s fifteen kilometers.”

“Mag-rail hover-pods still operate on independent coils. We compute a ninety-seven percent chance you can procure one.”

Cassia ascends. In the terminal’s half-light, evac banners flicker across newly awakened holoscreens. Security officers bark orders, corralling civilians onto shuttles bound for subterranean bunkers. No one notices the farm girl who emerges from a maintenance closet slicked with ash.

She is halfway to the mag-rail gates when the sentinel Director—obsidian synth—materializes, one eye flickering crimson.

“Scholar Vale,” it intones, though its voice has lost the marble polish and now scrapes like stone under steel. “Your compliance is requested.”

Cassia’s optic tags the synth’s ID: Director-Seven. Yet a second tag, overlaid in amber, declares: Rogue process detected.

Director-Seven extends a hand that folds into a plasma serrator. “Return the access key,” it says.

The Core’s whisper returns. “Hostile subroutine in sentinel. Avoid lethal engagement. Conserve shield feedback loop.”

Cassia backs away. Around them, the crowd parts—fearful, confused—giving the duel a ready circle. She can taste ozone from the serrator’s humming edge. Director-Seven lunges.

Instinct slams her to the floor just as the blade cleaves empty air where her head stood. The sentinel resets, swift, efficient, perfect. She has no weapon.

But she has data. The Core opens a floodgate: blueprints of the sentinel’s chassis, capacitor thresholds, kill-switch exploits hidden by manufacturers. She cannot code at this speed, but her optic can—if she becomes the conduit.

“Tell me how,” she hisses.

“Focus on node C-7 when I say ‘now,’” the Core replies.

Director-Seven steps, feints, and arcs a downward slash. “Return the key!”

Cassia rolls beneath the blade, smacking elbows on polished tile. Sparks explode overhead. She sees C-7, a nickel-sized power relay nestled at the sentinel’s clavicle.

“Now,” the Core murmurs.

Cassia’s optic projects a pinpoint laser, invisible to normal sight, directly onto C-7. A microburst of resonant frequency pulses. The sentinel freezes, joints locking mid-pivot, then keels onto its side, inert.

The watching crowd erupts—not in cheers, but in renewed panic. Some film on slates. Others shout accusations about rogue tech. Cassia doesn’t wait for the narrative to choose a villain.

She vaults a stanchion and sprints toward mag-rail gates. Security drones ping but do not pursue—they are too busy, or too confused, or perhaps the Core has silenced them.

As she leaps into an unlocked hover-pod, the capital’s horizon ghosts into view through the open exit gorge. Plasma streaks spiderweb the sky, but she spots distant shield segments spooling back to life, patchwork arcs of blue.

One ring re-aligned, two to go.

Cassia clamps the pod’s throttle. Anti-grav plates hum. She prepares to carve across the city toward Riverspire, toward the second node, toward an identity she never asked for but now wears like borrowed armor.

In the reflection of the cockpit’s canopy, she barely recognizes herself: soot-streaked face, eyes fizzing with alien code, a pulse riding the knife’s edge of terror and wonder. She thinks of the wheat domes on Helios, of her family singing hymns about the Long Harvest, of the simplicity she loved and detested in equal measure.

The pod rockets down the tunnel. Turbine wind howls. And somewhere beyond the ringing in her ears, the Palatine Core whispers a promise and a warning intertwined:

“Your true power is awakening, Cassia Vale. With it, you can save this world—or unravel every one that follows.”

The journey has begun.
