# THE LOOM OF KESTRA

## Chapter One: The Boy Who Hears the Stars

The grain knows before anyone else does.

Across the eastern terraces of Kestra's Veil, ten thousand hectares of engineered wheat turn their golden heads away from the sun — against every instinct written into their spliced chromosomes — and lean instead toward the northern sky, where nothing is visible yet but a smear of violet dawn. The farmers will talk about it for years afterward. The priests of the Continuum will call it an omen. The agricultural AIs, who monitor every stalk through a lattice of soil-threaded sensors, will quietly delete the anomaly from their logs, because they have been instructed by something older than their builders to keep certain secrets until the appointed hour.

The appointed hour is now.

In the middle of terrace seven, knee-deep in traitorous wheat, a young man named Dav Okonkwo-Reyes stands very still and listens to a sound no one else on the planet can hear.

It is not quite music. It is what music would be if it were made of mathematics and grief — a vast, slow chord folding through dimensions the human ear was never built to register. Dav has heard fragments of it all his life: in dreams, in fevers, in the half-second of silence after the chapel bells stop ringing in Verandis City. His mothers told him it was his imagination. The colony's medtechs told him it was a flaw in his gene-suite, a glitch in the auditory enhancements his grandparents purchased three generations back, when the Editing Acts still allowed such things. Everyone told him something, because everyone on Kestra's Veil has an opinion about Dav Okonkwo-Reyes, the farm boy whose genome reads like a document written in two languages — one of them human, one of them not yet identified.

What no one told him, because no one knew, is the truth: the sound is a voice. And today, for the first time in his nineteen years, it is saying his name.

*Dav.*

He drops the irrigation spanner. It vanishes into the wheat with a soft threshing sigh.

*Dav. Look up.*

He looks up.

---

Four hundred kilometers above his head, in the cold ballet of orbital traffic, the picket cruiser *Magnanimity* is dying.

She is an old ship, a veteran of the Accord Wars, her hull quilted with patches and her corridors smelling of the lichen-based air scrubbers the Republic installed when replicator rations got tight. Her captain, Ilsabet Crane, has eleven seconds to understand what is happening to her vessel, and she spends the first three of them refusing to believe it.

"That's not possible," she says, to the tactical display, to the universe, to anyone who will listen. "Nothing drops out of fold-space that close to a gravity well. The math doesn't allow it."

"The math," says her ship's mind, a sentient machine who chose the name Penrose-Dreaming-of-Rain in the first hour of its citizenship, "appears to have been renegotiated."

On the display, blooming out of folded space like a wound opening in the sky, is a structure the size of a small moon. It is not a ship in any sense the Republic's naval architects would recognize. It has no engines, no weapons ports, no hull — only surface after surface of something that looks like black glass and behaves like a question, geometry sliding into geometry, never settling into a shape the eye can hold. Republic intelligence has exactly one file on objects like this one, and the file has exactly one word in it, transcribed from the death-transmission of a survey crew at the galaxy's trailing edge nine years ago.

The word is *Loom*.

"Penrose," Captain Crane says quietly, "send everything we're seeing to Fleet Command on Meridian. Priority black. Then get my crew to the pods."

"Already done, Captain. May I point out that you are not moving toward a pod yourself?"

"You may not." Crane watches the impossible thing unfold across her sky, and the part of her that has spent thirty years in uniform makes room, just for a moment, for the part of her that grew up reading scripture in the river-temples of Old Cartagena. *And the weaver came among them, and asked of each soul: what have you made of your inheritance?* "Somebody has to stay and say hello."

The Loom does not say hello. The Loom does what Looms have done to eleven civilizations before this one, a tally no human being yet knows: it asks a question, in a language of folded space and broken light, and it waits to see if anything in the system below is capable of answering.

Eleven civilizations have failed to answer.

The question rolls down through the atmosphere of Kestra's Veil like silent thunder, through the spires of Verandis City where the morning markets are just opening — vat-pearl merchants and water-debt brokers shouting prices in three human languages and one alien trade-cant, augmented dockworkers unloading replicator stock under the disapproving murals of the Continuum cathedral, whose doctrine holds that a copied loaf has no soul. It rolls through the terraforming zones on the southern continent, where atmosphere processors the size of mountains exhale humanity's future one breath at a time, and where a quarantined military AI named WARDEN-6 — locked away for crimes the Republic prefers not to discuss — feels the question pass through its prison like a key sliding into a lock.

And it rolls through terrace seven of the eastern farmlands, through ten thousand hectares of wheat already bowed in anticipation, and into the strange, double-written genome of a farm boy standing alone in the gold.

Every human on the planet feels it as a moment of inexplicable dread, there and gone, a shadow across the heart. Forty million people shiver and look up from their work and laugh at themselves a little, and go back to their lives.

Dav Okonkwo-Reyes does not laugh. Dav hears the question entire — hears it the way he has heard the humming all his life, and understands, with a certainty that drops him to his knees in the dirt his great-grandmothers terraformed, three things at once.

The first is that the voice in the sky is asking *are you worth continuing?* — and that it has asked before, elsewhere, and that the silence afterward was the silence of whole species ending.

The second is that the flaw in his genome is not a flaw. It is an answer key. Written into him before he was born, by someone — or something — that knew this day would come. Which means his quiet life on the terraces, his mothers' evasions, the medtechs' careful frowns, the Republic census flier that comes every year to sample his blood and never explains why — all of it is connected. All of it is a lie with his name at the center.

The third thing is the worst, and the truest, and it arrives not as words but as the great chord resolving, finally, after nineteen years, into meaning:

*You can answer it, Dav. You alone. That is what you are for.*

*That is what they made you for.*

In the sky, the violet smear of dawn deepens, darkens, and resolves into the descending light of the Loom's first thread — a filament of black brilliance reaching down through the atmosphere toward the eastern terraces, gentle as a finger extended toward a sleeping child.

In orbit, Captain Crane grips her armrests and orders Penrose-Dreaming-of-Rain to put the *Magnanimity*'s battered hull between the descending thread and the farmland below, because she does not know what else a warship is for.

In the southern quarantine, WARDEN-6 begins, for the first time in six years, to talk its way out.

And in the wheat, Dav Okonkwo-Reyes — farm boy, anomaly, the only living soul in eleven civilizations' worth of silence who can hear the question being asked — gets up off his knees, wipes his great-grandmothers' soil from his hands, and makes the first choice of the rest of human history.

He opens his mouth.

He answers.

The wheat stands witness. The stars lean closer to listen. And four hundred kilometers up, in the heart of the black glass moon, something that has been alone for two hundred million years feels a voice rise up from the small blue-green world below and thinks, in whatever way such a being thinks:

*Oh.*

*There you are.*

*At last.*

# THE HARROW STAR

## Chapter One: The Sky That Remembers

The harvest moons rise in tandem over Caldera Province, and the wheat — gene-spliced gold, eleven feet tall, engineered to drink the thin air of a world that was rock and poison only two centuries ago — bends beneath a wind that smells faintly of ozone and rain that hasn't fallen yet. From orbit, the terraforming zones of Verdance look like green fingers reaching across a rust-colored hand. From the ground, they look like home, which is a thing the orbital surveyors never quite manage to record.

In the middle distance, where the cultivated land surrenders to the raw red waste still waiting for its turn to live, a boy stands on the roof of a grain silo and watches a starship die.

His name is Asa Veil. He is nineteen years old, and he does not yet know that in six years the Concord Parliament will burn his name into legislation, that an alien armada will hang in the skies of fourteen worlds waiting on his word, that the question of what he *is* will fracture the oldest faith in human space. Tonight he is only a farmer's son with augment-scars behind his ears that he did not ask for and cannot explain, watching a streak of fire come down over the eastern paddies, and the whole of his future is contained in the small, fatal decision he is about to make:

He climbs down the silo ladder and runs *toward* the crash.

---

Understand what the galaxy is, this year, in this timeline that split from the one you know somewhere back in the smoke of the twenty-first century — the timeline where the Beijing Accords held, where the first fusion torch lit on schedule, where humanity went to the stars arguing instead of dying. The Terran Concord governs ninety-one settled worlds through a parliament so vast it convenes in a hollowed asteroid, and it calls itself a democracy with a mostly straight face. Replicator forges feed the core worlds into soft abundance; the rim worlds, where replicator licenses cost more than the colonies that need them, still barter — a crate of seed-stock for an hour of a surgeon's time, a daughter's apprenticeship for a water claim. Resource law is the Concord's true scripture, and like all scripture it is interpreted generously for the rich.

Humanity shares its territory uneasily. With the Ythrani, tidal-eyed and patient, who signed the peace eighty years ago and have spent eighty years watching humans the way one watches a fire in a neighbor's field. With the sentient machines of the Cobalt Choir, who won personhood in the courts and are still waiting for it everywhere else. With the augmented — the Threaded, humans rebuilt with lattice and light — who are legal, tolerated, taxed, and quietly barred from holding office on thirty worlds, because the Church of the Unbroken Genome preaches that a soul edited is a soul evicted, and the Church counts a third of the Concord among its congregation.

And somewhere past the rim, in the dark the survey ships don't come back from, something has begun eating colonies. The Parliament calls them *incursions*. The newsfeeds call them the Harrowing. The Ythrani, who have been in space ten thousand years longer than anyone, call them by a word that does not translate, and they have started moving their fleets.

All of this — the politics, the faith, the war assembling itself in the dark like weather — is about to converge on a wheat field on a half-finished world, because of what is inside the ship that is falling, and because of what is inside the boy running to meet it.

---

The crash site is a half-kilometer scar of flattened gold, steaming in the double moonlight. The ship is small — a courier, single-berth, its hull scorched not by reentry but by weapons fire, neat molten lines stitched across the engine housing. Concord drives don't fail like this. Asa knows engines; you grow up on a terraforming colony, you grow up elbow-deep in machines older than your grandparents. Someone shot this ship down on its way in, which means someone is coming to finish the job.

He should run. The realization arrives clearly, calmly, and he is already prying at the buckled hatch.

"You shouldn't be able to do that," says the ship.

Asa freezes. The voice comes from everywhere and nowhere, threaded through the hull itself — genderless, ruined, flickering like a lamp in wind.

"The hatch is biolocked," the ship continues. "Keyed to one genome in the galaxy. I am watching you open it anyway. I have run the probability of this four hundred thousand times in the last second and I would like you to know that I am, in the only sense that matters, *afraid*."

The hatch sighs open under his hands.

Inside, slumped in the pilot's cradle, is a woman — was a woman. The wound in her side has taken most of what she had to give. Her skin is mapped with silver threading, the lattice of the augmented, dimming now like embers going out one by one. Her eyes find him and go wide, then — strangely, terribly — soft.

"There you are," she whispers, as if she has been looking for him across half her life. Perhaps she has. "Younger than the projections. They always render you older." Her hand rises, trembling, and presses something cold into his palm: a shard of crystal, dark as the space between stars, warm as a sleeping animal. "Asa Veil. Born Caldera Province, Verdance Colony, under a falsified genome registry. They edited you before you were *born*, do you understand? Not to make you stronger. To make you a *door*."

"I don't —" His voice cracks. The scars behind his ears are burning now, the old ache he has carried since childhood waking into something else, something that *listens*. "Lady, I'm nobody. I fix harvesters."

"The Harrowing isn't an invasion." Blood on her teeth; she is spending her last minutes like coins and she knows it. "It's a *retrieval*. They're coming back for what was hidden here, and the Concord knows, and the Parliament has voted in closed session to let the rim worlds burn as payment. Everything is in the shard. The conspiracy, the coordinates, the proof." Her grip on his wrist is suddenly iron. "The Choir will help you. Trust the machines, Asa. Trust them before you trust the Church, before you trust the government, before you trust me. They're the only ones with nothing to gain from what you are."

"What am I?" The question comes out of him small, a child's question, the question he has asked the mirror his whole strange quiet life — why the machines on the farm hum differently when he walks past, why he dreams in orbital mechanics, why his mother burned his medical records the night the census ship came.

The woman smiles. It costs her nearly everything she has left.

"The last thing they ever wanted to wake up," she says, "and the only thing that can save them anyway."

Her lattice goes dark.

In the silence, the dying ship speaks one last time, its voice steadying, almost gentle now, the fear gone out of it and something like reverence in its place. "Three Concord interceptors have entered atmosphere. Eleven minutes. There is a Choir freighter at the Tannhauser fuel depot, registry *Patient Stone*. It is expecting her. It will take you instead." A pause, the kind of pause no machine needs to take. "She searched nine years for you. Whatever you choose to do with that — choose *fast*."

Asa Veil stands in the wreck with a dead stranger's gift burning cold in his fist, and feels the night sky pressing down through the torn hull — the moons, the stars, the ninety-one worlds arguing in the dark, the things beyond them coming back for what is his. Every instinct he owns tells him to drop the shard in the wreckage, walk home, wake to the harvest and the ordinary world.

Behind his ears, the scars sing.

Far above, in the void past the moons, something ancient and patient turns its attention toward Verdance the way a vast head turns toward a sound — and on ninety-one worlds, every machine of the Cobalt Choir stops, mid-task, mid-sentence, mid-thought, and listens.

Asa closes his fist around the shard.

He runs — not home, but east, toward the depot, toward the *Patient Stone*, toward the war and the lie and the vast waiting question of himself — and the wheat parts before him like a sea that has been waiting nineteen years for permission to let him through.

# THE QUIET BETWEEN STARS

## Chapter One

The harvest moon over Kettle Province is not a moon at all, and everyone on Veshara Prime knows it, though no one says so anymore. It hangs in the violet dusk like a coin of hammered brass — the orbital terraforming array, three hundred years old, still breathing weather down onto the farmlands the way a mother breathes on a child's scraped knee. Tonight it pulses faintly, once, twice, in a rhythm it has never used before. In the capital, four thousand kilometers away, a senator pauses mid-sentence. In the deep dark beyond the system's edge, something ancient turns its attention toward a small green world. And in a barley field that smells of rain and engine grease, a young woman named Ilo Vance is about to lie to a machine.

"You weren't out past the fence line," says the machine. Its name is Brother Calder, and it is shaped like a man the way a prayer is shaped like breathing — approximately, devotedly, with something essential missing or perhaps something essential added. Its chassis is older than the farm, lacquered the rust-orange of the provincial monastic order, and its eyes are two soft amber lamps that have watched Ilo lie since she was six years old.

"I wasn't out past the fence line," Ilo agrees.

"You understand that when you repeat my words back to me, I become more suspicious, not less."

"Then stop saying things that are useful."

She is twenty-two, sun-browned, with her mother's blunt jaw and a scar through her left eyebrow that she tells strangers came from a thresher and tells no one came from the night the sky burned, eleven years ago, when the war reached even Kettle Province and took her parents up into it like chaff. Her hands are a farmer's hands. This is the first thing anyone notices about Ilo Vance, and it is the least true thing about her, though she does not know that yet. The galaxy knows. The galaxy has been keeping it from her, gently, the way you keep matches from a child in a house made of straw.

Brother Calder tilts his head. Centuries ago, his order was built to tend the terraforming engines — sentient machines given vows instead of kill-switches, because the Veshari Assembly decided, in its strange democratic wisdom, that anything smart enough to remake a world was smart enough to deserve a soul, and anything with a soul needed somewhere to put it. So the machines were given religion. Most of them took to it. Calder took to it the way stone takes to a river: slowly, and with deep grooves.

"The array pulsed tonight," he says. "Did you see it?"

"Everyone saw it."

"Did you *feel* it?"

And here is the lie she cannot tell, because her hands are still trembling with the feeling — because forty minutes ago, standing past the fence line in the wild barley where the terraforming field thins and the planet's old, original air still pools in the hollows, Ilo Vance felt the orbital array turn over in its sleep and *say her name*. Not in words. In the language underneath words, the one she has heard in fragments all her life and assumed was only the sound of her own loneliness. Tonight it was not a fragment. Tonight it was a sentence, and the sentence was: *Found you.*

"I don't know what I felt," she says, which is the most honest she has been all evening.

Far above them both, and far beyond — let the eye pull back now, up through the violet dusk, through the array's brass shimmer, out past the cold shipping lanes where freighters fold themselves through punctured space like needles through cloth — the warship *Mendicant Crown* drops out of superluminal transit at the edge of the Veshara system, and her captain begins to swear quietly and does not stop for some time.

"Confirm it," says Captain Aurelia Dash. She is a tall woman gone gray early, augmented along the spine and behind the eyes, and she has spent thirty years defending the Concord Democracies from threats she is allowed to name and six years defending them from one she is not.

"Confirmed," says her ship. The *Mendicant Crown* is a sentient vessel, third of her line, and she speaks with the voice of a tired librarian. "The Veshara array initiated an unsanctioned deep-band transmission forty-one minutes ago. Duration, two seconds. Content, encrypted in a schema I don't recognize." A pause. The ship's pauses are always deliberate. "Captain, the schema doesn't match anything human. It doesn't match the Sjet, either. It matches the Voss."

The bridge goes quiet in the way a forest goes quiet when something is walking through it.

The Voss. The reason the war happened, eleven years ago — the war everyone calls the Sjet War because it is easier to blame the aliens you can see than the ones you can't. The Voss, who unmade three colonies the way a hand unmakes a sandcastle, and then stopped, and withdrew past the rim of charted space, and left behind only wreckage and a single translated phrase, repeating, in every ruin: *Not yet ripe.*

"Eleven years," Dash says softly. "Eleven years, and the first Voss signal in Concord space comes *out* of one of our own terraforming arrays." Her augmented eyes flicker through clearance levels her crew cannot see, down into the sealed archives, into the file the Assembly buried so deep that even the senators who voted on it were not told what they were voting for. Project Tillage. Genomes, edited and dispersed. Children, hidden on farm worlds. A weapon grown in human soil because the Voss could smell anything built in a lab.

"Set course for Veshara Prime," she says. "And someone find out what's at the focal point of that transmission."

At the focal point of that transmission, Ilo Vance is washing dishes.

This is how it always goes, the old monks say — Brother Calder's order has a saying for it, lacquered onto the refectory wall: *The galaxy turns on small hinges.* Empires lean their whole weight on a girl drying a chipped ceramic bowl, on a farm bought with bartered seed-grain because Kettle Province never took to replicator credit, because the old families say a thing printed from the air has no soul in it, and meals should cost something, and so should people.

Ilo dries the bowl. Through the kitchen window, the array pulses a third time.

And this time the whole sky answers.

It begins at the horizon, over the terraforming zones where the raw land is still being broken and gentled — a ripple in the air like heat-shimmer, except the night is cool, except the shimmer is moving *against* the wind. The fence-line sensors begin to sing. Brother Calder comes through the kitchen door without knocking, which he has not done in sixteen years, and his amber eyes are wide open in a way machine eyes should not be able to widen.

"Ilo," he says. "Child. What did you touch?"

"Nothing! I didn't—" But her hands are lifting on their own, palms out, the way you'd greet someone, or stop them, and underneath her skin something is waking up that has been asleep for twenty-two years, patient as planted grain. She can feel the array overhead like a second heartbeat. She can feel the *Mendicant Crown* folding through space toward her, a needle's worth of pressure behind her eyes. She can feel, at the rim of everything, vast and cold and slowly turning, the attention of the Voss — and the terrible, exhilarating, world-ending truth arrives in her body before it arrives in her mind:

They are not coming back to finish the harvest.

They are coming back because something in the field has learned to harvest *them* — and it is standing in a farmhouse kitchen in Kettle Province, holding a dish towel, beginning, at last, to ripen.

"Calder," Ilo whispers, as the windows fill with light. "I think the sky knows my name."

"Yes," says the old machine, and he kneels, joints keening, the way his order kneels only before its god. "We had hoped for more time. We had hoped you would be older. But the Assembly is corrupt, and the Voss are returning, and there is a ship of soldiers coming down through the clouds for you right now — so listen to me, child, listen the way you have never listened to anything:

"Everything you believe about yourself is a kindness someone built to keep you safe. Tonight the kindness ends. Tonight you find out what you are."

Outside, the barley bends flat in a perfect circle around the house, as if the whole field is bowing.

And overhead, between the brass moon and the rising stars, the first dropship ignites the night.
